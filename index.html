Introduction

Helicopters rely on rotating blades to generate lift, making the rotor blade design central to their performance. This is unlike aeroplanes which rely on forward motion to push air over fixed wings. These blades act like rotating wings, constantly moving through the air to generate lift and allow the aircraft to hover, climb, and manoeuvre in ways that fixed wing aircraft cannot. Because of this, rotor blade design plays a central role in determining how well a helicopter performs. Engineers need to balance aerodynamic efficiency, structural strength, vibration control, safety, and cost.

Airfoil shape

The most basic part of rotor blade design is the Airfoil shape, the curved cross-section of the blade. As the blade spins, its airfoil shape creates the pressure difference needed to generate lift. Different shapes are used depending on the type of helicopter. Thicker airfoils are strong and produce good lift at lower speeds. Thinner airfoils create less drag, making them more efficient at high speeds. Symmetrical airfoils, which have the same shape on the top and bottom, make the helicopter easier to control. Choosing the right airfoil is a compromise between strength, lift, and drag. A well designed airfoil reduces the amount of engine power required, improving fuel efficiency and payload capability.

Aspect Ratio

Aspect ratio refers to the relationship between blade length and blade width. Long, narrow blades have a high aspect ratio and are more aerodynamically efficient. Many modern helicopters use high aspect ratio blades because they reduce induced drag, especially in hover. However, longer blades also introduce challenges such as requiring stronger materials to withstand greater bending forces. Longer blades also risk reaching the speed of sound at the tips, which causes dangerous compressibility effects. As a result, designers must carefully balance efficiency with mechanical limitations and performance requirements.
